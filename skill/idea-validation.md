# Idea Validation

## Purpose

Most startups fail because they build products nobody wants.

The goal of idea validation is to determine whether a problem is worth solving before investing significant time, money, or resources.

This framework helps founders systematically evaluate startup ideas before moving into product development.

---

# Validation Framework

Evaluate ideas across five dimensions:

1. Problem
2. Customer
3. Market
4. Timing
5. Distribution

---

# 1. Problem Validation

## Key Questions

* What problem exists?
* Who experiences this problem?
* How frequently does it occur?
* How painful is it?
* What happens if it remains unsolved?

## Strong Signals

* Users actively complain about it.
* Users already spend time solving it.
* Users already spend money solving it.
* Existing solutions are inefficient.

## Weak Signals

* The problem sounds interesting.
* The founder personally likes the idea.
* The technology is exciting.

# 2. Customer Validation

## Define ICP

Ideal Customer Profile:

* Who are they?
* What do they do?
* What motivates them?
* What frustrates them?
* What alternatives do they currently use?

## Validation Methods

### Interviews

Target:

* 15–30 conversations

Questions:

* What are your biggest challenges?
* How do you currently solve them?
* What is missing?
* What would an ideal solution look like?

### Observation

Watch users:

* Workflows
* Tools
* Behaviors
* Pain points

---

# 3. Market Validation

## TAM Framework

### Total Addressable Market

How large is the opportunity?

### Serviceable Market

Which portion can realistically be served?

### Obtainable Market

What can be captured within 3 years?

---

## Market Signals

Strong:

* Growing category
* Increasing demand
* New infrastructure unlocks
* Expanding user base

Weak:

* Declining market
* Saturated category
* No user demand

---

# 4. Competitive Analysis

## Questions

* Who are the direct competitors?
* Who are the indirect competitors?
* What advantages do they have?
* What weaknesses exist?

## Rule

Competition validates demand.

The goal is not to avoid competitors.

The goal is to differentiate.

---

# 5. Solana Relevance Test

Not every startup needs to be on Solana.

Ask:

* Does Solana create a better experience?
* Does blockchain add value?
* Can the product exist without crypto?
* Does decentralization matter?

## Strong Solana Use Cases

* Digital ownership
* Payments
* On-chain identity
* Creator economies
* Financial products
* Global marketplaces

## Weak Solana Use Cases

* Blockchain added only for marketing.
* No user benefit from on-chain infrastructure.

# Validation Decision Framework

Proceed if:

* Problem is painful.
* Users acknowledge the problem.
* Market opportunity exists.
* Clear differentiation exists.
* Solana adds meaningful value.

Do Not Proceed if:

* Demand is unverified.
* Customers are unclear.
* Differentiation is weak.
* Solana adds no advantage.

# Field Notes

## Distribution Is Not Demand

Many crypto products mistake incentive-driven activity for product-market fit.

A project can have:

* Thousands of users
* Large TVL
* Strong social engagement

and still have weak demand.

When incentives disappear, true demand becomes visible.

Always separate:

* Organic users
* Incentivized users

---

## Founders Usually Build Too Early

Most founders start with a solution.

The best founders start with a problem.

The question is not:

"Can we build this?"

The question is:

"Should this exist?"

---

## User Interviews Beat Twitter Polls

Social media creates opinions.

User interviews reveal behavior.

Behavior is usually more valuable than opinions.
